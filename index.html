Alkenes

X

  • Boiling/melting points a few degrees below alkanes
  • CnH2n
  • Non-polar

Alkynes

X

  • Boiling/melting points slightly higher than alkanes or alkenes
  • CnH2n-2
  • Non-polar

Haloalkanes

X

  • Prefixes include "Fluro", "Chloro", "Diio","Tribromo", etc.
  • Halogen bonded to alkyl chain (e.g. F, Cl, Br, or I)
  • Higher boiling point than Alkanes
  • More Halogens: higher boiling point, bigger Halogens: higher boiling point

Sugars

X

  • Glucose and Fructose are the most common sugars, they break down to form ethanol
  • Sucrose (table sugar) is a molecule of Glucose and Fructose
  • Cellulose is a polymer of Glucose, and can be broken down much more slowly
  • Glucose and Fructose are soluble in water

Alcohols

X

  • Suffix -al
  • If not highest functional group, add as 'hydroxy'
  • Liquids at room temperature
  • Polar
  • Becomes less soluble as chain length increases

Aldehydes

X

  • Suffix -al
  • Reducing agents (and easily oxidised)
  • Methanal and Ethanal are gasses at room temperature, all others are liquids at room temperature
  • Polar

Ketones

X

  • Suffix -one
  • Reducing agent
  • Liquids at room temperature
  • Polar

Carboxylic acids

X

  • Suffic -oic acid (e.g. ethanoic acid)
  • High boiling points (methanoic acid = 101 °C)
  • Formic acid = Methanoic acid
  • Acetic acid = Ethanoic acid
  • Weak acids
  • Polar
  • Soluble in water

Esters

X

  • Are what gives many things scent
  • Named in format [R chain]yl [R' chain]oate, e.g Methyl Propanoate
  • Are liquids at room temperature (Methyl Methanoate boils at 31.8 °C
  • Polar, soluble in water

Carboxylate ions

X

  • Conjugate bases to carboxylic acids
  • Electron effectively gets 'shared' between two Oxygens
  • Suffix -oate ion (e.g. 'butanoate ion')
  • Methanoate = formate, ethanoate = acetate

Amides

X

  • Suffix -amide
  • Any chains coming of the Nitrogen are have prefix N (e.g. N-methylpentanamide)
  • High boiling points
  • Polar, soluble in water
  • primary/secondary/tertiary determined by how many Carbons attached to Nitrogen

Amines

X

  • Suffix -amine
  • Methylamine (-6.3 °C) and Ethylamine (16.6 °C) gases at room temperature, higher chain lengths are liquids
  • primary/secondary/tertiary determined by how many Carbons attached to Nitrogen

Halogenation of Alkenes

X

Requires:

  • Hydrogen-Halogen or diatomic Halogen

When an alkene is exposed to a Hydrogen-Halogen or a diatomic Halogen molecule it reacts to form a Haloalkane.

Hydration of alkenes

X

Requires:

  • Concentrated strong acid (e.g. H2SO4)
  • Cooling
  • Distillation setup
  • Alkene bigger than butene

With the presense of a concentrated strong acid (e.g. H2SO4) a mixture of a liquid alkene and water will produce an alcohol. Pentene is the first alkene to have be liquid at room-temperature (boils at 30°C), so this method will not work with smaller alkenes. The reaction is exothermic, so to make it happen cool the mixture.

The mixture of alcohol and alkene can be distilled, as alcohols have a much lower boiling point.

Hydrolysis of haloalkanes (to alcohols)

X

In the presence of a strong base (OH-) and heat a haloalkane will react to form an alcohol.The haloalkane has a lower boiling point than the alcohol, so a reflux setup will be used.

Alcohols to Haloalkanes

X

When treated with with a Hydrogen-Halogen molecule an alcohol will form a haloalkane. This is too slow for Chloroalkanes, so a different method is used for them.

Primary alcohols and ammonia

X

Requires

:
  • Ammonia
  • Silica or other catalyst
  • 150°C - 220°C

Ammonia is basic, and so would usually pick up a proton to form ammonium rather than react with an alcohol, however in the temperature range 150°C - 220°C they do react.

Dehydration of alcohols

X

When an alcohol is heated in the presence of a strong acid it will react to form an alkane. This can be extracted using a distillation setup.

Secondary alcohol oxidation (to Ketones)

X

Secondary alcohols can be oxidised to ketones in the presence of a strong acid (e.g. H2SO4) using a catalyst such as Dichromate. A distilling setup is used for this reaction.

Ester synthesis (alcohol + carboxylic acid)

X

Requires:

  • Alcohol
  • Carboxylic acid
  • Heat
  • Strong, conc. acid (e.g. H2SO4)
  • Reflux setup

An alcohol and a carboxylic acid heated under reflux in the presence of a strong concentrated acid will form an ester. A reflux condenser is needed to ensure reactants stay in system.

Primary alcohol oxidation (to carboxylic acid)

X

Requires

  • Oxidising catalyst (e.g. KMnO4 or H2CrO4)
  • Heat
  • Water
  • Reflux setup

When an alcohol in the presence of a catalyst is heated under reflux the reaction will continue until the alcohol is turned into a carboxylic acid.

Primary alcohol oxidation (to aldehyde)

X

Requires:

  • Catalyst (e.g. Cr2O72-)
  • Distillation setup

Distillation removes the aldehyde from the reaction so it does not continue through to Carboxylic acid.

Fermentation

X

The fermentation of sugars is a biological process. Bacteria or fungi such as yeast break down glucose into ethanol. Enzymes such as Maltase can catalyse the reaction to break down other sugars into glucose.

Aldehyde oxidation (to carboxylic acid)

X

Requires:

  • Oxidising catalyst (e.g. KMnO4 or H2CrO4)
  • Heat
  • Water
  • Reflux setup

When an aldehyde is heated under reflux in the precense of a catalyst it will react to form a carboxylic acid.

Carboxylic acid to Carboxylate ion

X

Requires:

  • Base (OH-)

When a carboxylic acid and a base neutralise each other, a carboxylate ion is a product.

Amide synthesis (carboxylic acid and amine)

X

Requires:

  • Lots of heat or DCC as activating agent

To do this reaction without DCC lots of heat (>100°C) is required to prevent the amine from neutralising the carboxylic acid and making an unreactive carboxylate ion.
